Statistics of Slaves, 1790-1900 (1909)

...The higher percentage shown for the decade 1800 to 1810 reflects the large importation of negroes during the years immediately preceding January 1, 1808, after which date the international trade in slaves was prohibited in the United States...the growth in the number of slaves hereafter can be attributed to the domestic slave trade and rapid forced population increase amongst the slaves.

...Delaware, Maryland and Virginia were principally in the cultivation of tobacco in early years. The soil was rapidly impoverished by this crop, and as a result the center of tobacco culture tended to move farther westward, into new and more favorable sections of Kentucky and Tennessee.

As the cultivation of tobacco by slave labor became somewhat less profitable in the older states, the acquisition of new territory in the far South (Georgia, Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas, and Florida) and the introduction of and rapid expansion of cotton growing in that section made slave labor highly profitable in connection with this important crop. After further importation of slaves was prohibited in 1808, the market price of negroes advanced rapidly, because of the increasing demand for their services in the cotton fields...
