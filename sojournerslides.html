Key Word Focus
ABOLITIONIST
Unit Vocabulary

What Is An Abolitionist?

Break It Down
  • ABOLISH = to end something unfair or wrong.
  • ABOLITION = the ending of slavery.
  • -IST = a person who believes in or fights for something.

ABOLITIONIST = a person who wants slavery to end and works against it.

In This Unit

  • Sojourner Truth is an abolitionist because she speaks out against slavery.
  • Other abolitionists use speeches, newspapers, and organizing to fight slavery.
  • When you see the word abolitionist, think: “person fighting to end slavery.”

Setting The Scene
Akron, Ohio · 1851
Before The Speech

Who Is In The Room?

Picture The Scene
  • The year is 1851, before the Civil War. Slavery is still legal in the South.
  • In the North, people are arguing about slavery and about women’s rights.
  • In Akron, Ohio, a Women’s Rights Convention is happening in a crowded hall.
  • There are white women, white men, abolitionists, church people, and some who think women should stay quiet.
  • Into this room walks Sojourner Truth, a Black woman who used to be enslaved and now travels to speak for freedom and equality.

This is the audience she is facing when she stands up and gives her speech "Ain't I a Woman?"

Map & Sectionalism
Where Is Akron?
North vs South

Akron In A Divided Country

Look At The Map

Find Akron on this map of the United States.

Notice that Akron is in Ohio, near the Great Lakes, in the free North.

  • Before the Civil War, the country is splitting into two big sections: states that allow slavery and states that do not.
  • Ohio is a free state, part of the Northern states that are arguing against the spread of slavery.
  • People in places like Akron are caught in debates about slavery, race, and women’s rights.

Audience Clues One
Part C
Question 1

What Does Her Audience Think?

“I have as much muscle as any man, and can do as much work as any man.”

On Your Worksheet

  1. 1a. What does this line tell you about what some people in the audience might think about women?
  2. 1b. Who is she trying to prove something to here: men, women, both, or someone else? Explain.

Use complete sentences. Think about what kind of stereotypes she is arguing against.

Audience Clues Two
Part C
Question 2

Minds, Rights, And Everyday Life

“As for intellect, all I can say is, if women have a pint and man a quart, why can’t she have her little pint full?”
  1. 2a. What is she saying about women’s minds and rights in this line?
  2. 2b. What kind of audience would understand this joke about “pint” and “quart”?

Hint: Think about who uses pints and quarts in daily life, and what it means to be “full.”

Audience Clues Three and Four
Part C
Questions 3 · 4

Beliefs, The Bible, And Direct Talk

“I cant read, but I can hear. I have heard the bible and have learned that Eve caused man to sin.”
  1. 3a. What does this tell you about the audience’s beliefs or what they care about?
  2. 3b. Why do you think she brings up the Bible when she talks to this audience?
“And how came Jesus into the world? Through God who created him and woman who bore him. Man, where is your part?”
  1. 4a. Who is she speaking directly to in this line?
  2. 4b. What is she trying to make this audience think or feel?

Finish all of Part C on your worksheet. Use evidence from the lines to support your answers.
